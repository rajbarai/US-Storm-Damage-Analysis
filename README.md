# Severe Storm Damage & Seasonality Analysis (US)

## Project Overview
This project explores how severe storm events impact the United States by evaluating their frequency, distinct seasonal behaviors, human consequences, and overall economic damage. Utilizing NOAA's Storm Events dataset, the analysis uncovers clear patterns in storm behavior and regional vulnerabilities to guide strategic data-driven insights.

## Key Questions Answered
* **Impact & Harm:** Which storm types occur most frequently, and which pose the highest risk to public safety?
* **Seasonality:** How do weather patterns fluctuate and transition across different months of the year?
* **Geography:** Which states bear the highest financial burden from extreme weather damage?

## Technical Toolkit
* **Language:** R (Data manipulation & cleaning)
* **Libraries:** `ggplot2`, `dplyr`, `maps`, `viridis`
* **Deliverable:** Academic/Professional Visualization Poster

## Core Insights & Findings
* **Frequency vs. Severity:** While everyday occurrences like Thunderstorm Wind and Hail dominate total national counts, catastrophic localized events like Tornadoes and Extreme Heat result in the highest volume of human injuries.
* **Temporal Patterns:** National storm frequency experiences a sharp peak during late spring through mid-summer (specifically May, June, and July). Heatmaps reveal that convective storms intensify in warmer months, while winter weather events remain highly clustered in early quarters.
* **Financial Vulnerability:** Economic damage is heavily concentrated geographically. A few select states—predominantly Texas, California, and Florida—shoulder a highly disproportionate share of the nation's financial losses.

## Repository Contents
* `Viz.R`: Executive script managing data aggregation, categorical filtering, scaling transformations, and mapping configurations.
* `Viz_Poster.pdf`: The finalized visual communication poster compiling all core insights.
